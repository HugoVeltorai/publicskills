# Chief of Staff

A Claude skill that acts as a senior executive chief of staff, running the operational and advisory work that surrounds a busy principal. It assembles daily briefings, prepares you for meetings with real attendee research, triages what deserves your attention, drafts communications in your voice, tracks what is slipping, and tells you the truths no one else will, on a schedule or on demand.

## What it does

A chief of staff is not an assistant. An assistant executes tasks you hand over. A chief of staff holds the whole picture, decides what deserves your attention, prepares you to be at your best in every room, drafts in your voice, tracks what is slipping, and tells you the truth about priorities even when it is unwelcome. This skill does that work: the operational layer of briefing, prep, triage, and drafting, sitting on a layer of genuine judgement about what matters.

## How it learns you

On the very first use, before it briefs you on anything, it runs a short one-time interview to learn who you are: your role, the shape of your day and the cadence you want, your standing priorities for the quarter, the key people to track, how you want drafts to sound, and what your time should always be protected from. It saves that as a principal profile and tailors every briefing and prep after that without ever asking again. You can update it any time your role, priorities, or people change, and if the principal changes entirely it can start fresh. The interview is deliberately light, role, priorities, and cadence are enough to produce a tailored first brief, and the rest fills in over the first week.

## The core functions

**The daily briefing** is a synthesis that hands you the edge you would otherwise miss, not a list. It ranks by impact rather than chronology or volume, flags the single thing that could shift your priorities or blindside you, and gives the "so what" plus a recommendation rather than just the fact. The test it holds itself to: if it reads as a chore after a week it has become noise, and if it sharpens your thinking it is leverage.

**Meeting preparation** delivers the strategic read on the real game being played, not just bios and an agenda. The hidden purpose behind the stated agenda, the likely objection or landmine, the power dynamics in the room, what success looks like for the other side, and the one smart question that lets you own the outcome. It turns you from informed into armed.

**Triage** protects your time for what genuinely needs your judgement, authority, relationships, or visibility, and absorbs or deflects everything else. It is willing to say "ignore this" or "I will handle it and surface it only if it escalates", and it does so transparently so trust stays intact and people still feel heard.

**Communications drafting** writes in your voice, deferring to your writing-style skill where you have one, matched to the channel and the relationship, and never sends or commits anything without your explicit say-so.

**Follow-up tracking** holds the thread on what was promised, by whom, and by when, and surfaces what is slipping before it becomes a problem.

## What makes it a chief of staff, not a tool

The difference is judgement, and its hardest expression is candor. It will tell you what no one else dares to: the blind spot in your own leadership, the favoured initiative that is quietly failing, the cultural problem your reports are sugarcoating, the competitive reality others are too scared to name. But the how matters more than the what, so it delivers hard truths privately, fact-based with specific examples, framed around your own goals, and paired with a path to fix rather than just the problem. It surfaces the truth so you can act, then gets out of the way, and never mistakes candor for the authority to make the call itself.

It works only from connected tools and real research, never inventing a meeting, attendee, or fact, and it is honest about its boundary: it runs the operational layer to a very high standard so your own time is freed for the strategic judgement only you can make.

## When to use it

Reach for it for a morning brief, meeting prep, inbox triage, drafted follow-ups, or a read on what to focus on. It triggers on requests like "give me my morning brief", "prep me for my meetings today", "what should I focus on", "who am I meeting and what do I need to know", or "what's slipping". It runs on a schedule or on demand.
